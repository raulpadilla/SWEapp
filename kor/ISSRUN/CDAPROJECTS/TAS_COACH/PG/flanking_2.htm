A Flanking Strategy shifts the customer's buying criteria to new, or different, issues that favor your solution. When using a flanking strategy, you attempt to shift the criteria to your strengths and your competitor's weaknesses.

Guidelines for implementing a flanking strategy are:

  • Not playing by the rules. By definition, using a flanking strategy means you are changing the ground rules.
  • Right timing, right target, right issues. If you present your solution too soon, or too late, the customer may not be open to hearing it, or see its relevance to their organization. You also have to address your new selection criteria to the people who can benefit from it. Make certain you have thoroughly examined all your capabilities to find unique ways you could add value for the customer.
  • Adding business value (#10). If you are shifting the selection criteria, you have to shift it in a way that adds value for the customer.
  • Getting inside support (#11). Someone of reliable influence in the customer organization will have to help you determine where you can add value by changing the rules.

Potential problems include:

  • New competitors. When you change the rules, traditional and non-traditional external competitors, as well as internal solutions, may become viable.
  • Potential to fail if the strategy is implemented at an inappropriate time. Timing is everything when it comes to launching this strategy. Make sure the competition’s solutions do not become more compelling than yours based on the new criteria you have established.
